Inverse Trigonometric Derivatives
The derivative of `f(x)` can be defined as `\frac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}`.
Reflecting across the line `y=x` to produce the inverse,
the new slope can be written as `\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}y}`, thus following that
`\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}y} \cdot \frac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=1` and `\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}y} f^{-1}(y) \cdot \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x} f(x) = 1`,
also said that the derivatives of inverse functions are multiplicative inverses.
Alternatively, `f^{-1}(f(x))=x`, and it follows with
the chain rule that `\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}f^{-1}(f(x)) \cdot \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}f(x) = 1`.

For example, let `f(x)=\sin(x)` and `f^{-1}(x)=\sin^{-1}(x)`, hence
`\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}f^{-1}(\sin(x)) = \frac{1}{\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}\sin(x)} = \frac{1}{\cos(x)}`
Remember that `\sin^2(x)+\cos^2(x)=1`, so `\cos(x)=\sqrt{1-\sin^2(x)}`.
`\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}f^{-1}(\sin(x)) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\sin^2(x)}}`
Replace `\sin(x)` with `x` in the left and right, and `f^{-1}(x)` with `\sin^{-1}(x)`:
`\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}\sin^{-1}(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}`
