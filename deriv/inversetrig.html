Inverse Trigonometric Derivatives
Given the `x` and `y` definitions, the derivative of f(x) can be defined as `\frac{dy}{dx}`.
Reflecting across the line `y=x` to produce the inverse,
the new slope can be written as `\frac{dx}{dy}`, thus following that
`\frac{dx}{dy} * \frac{dy}{dx}=1` and `\frac{d}{dy} f^{-1}(y) * \frac{d}{dx} f(x) = 1`,
also said that the derivatives of inverse functions are multiplicative inverses.
Alternatively, `f^{-1}(f(x))=x`, and it follows with
the chain rule that `\frac{d}{dx}f^{-1}(f(x)) * \frac{d}{dx}f(x) = 1`.

For example, let `f(x)=sin(x)` and `f^{-1}(x)=sin^{-1}(x)`, hence
`\frac{d}{dx}f^{-1}(sin(x)) = \frac{1}{\frac{d}{dx}sin(x)}`.
`\frac{d}{dx}f^{-1}(sin(x)) = \frac{1}{cos(x)}`
Remember that `sin^2(x)+cos^2(x)=1`, so `cos(x)=\sqrt{1-sin^2(x)}`.
`\frac{d}{dx}f^{-1}(sin(x)) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-sin^2(x)}}`
Replace `sin(x)` with `x` in the left and right, and `f^{-1}(x)` with `sin^{-1}(x)`:
`\frac{d}{dx}sin^{-1}(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}`
