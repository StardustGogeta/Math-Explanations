Rational Roots Theorem
The rational root theorem states that for any polynomial of integer coefficients
`y=a_0x^n+a_1x^{n-1}+\ldots+a_n`,
all rational roots (zeros) can be expressed as `\frac pq` where `p` and `q` are integer factors of `a_n` and `a_0`, respectively.

To see this, break the polynomial into a linear factorization, such as
`y=(a_0x+c_0)(a_1x+c_1)(a_2x+c_2)\ldots`
Each zero of the function is of the form `\frac{c_n}{a_n}`, and there exist no other zeros.
When multiplying each term together, the resulting function will be
`y=x^n(a_0a_1a_2\ldots) + \ldots + (c_0c_1c_2\ldots)`
Thus, it is clear that the coefficient of the first term is the product of the denominators, and the final, constant term is the product of the numerators.
As such, all possible pairs of `p` and `q` are permutations of the factors of the constant and the first coefficient, respectively.
